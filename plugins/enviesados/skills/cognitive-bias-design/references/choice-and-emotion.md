# Choice & emotion — Decision Fatigue, Choice Overload, Affect Heuristic

> Chapters 7 and 8 of Rian Dutra, *ENVIESADOS* (2024).
> Chapter 7 is about the **cost** of deciding. Chapter 8 is about what we fall back on when that
> cost gets too high.

---

## 1. Decision Fatigue & Choice Overload — "too many choices and we can't decide"

### Mechanism

Every decision — important or not — costs mental effort. We make roughly **35,000 conscious
decisions a day**, and **200+ about food alone**. Repeated, this produces fatigue from mental
exhaustion. Fatigue also comes from situations demanding excess self-control (controlling emotions,
intense cognitive effort).

The reframe that matters for design:

> Users may look lazy and impatient — "mas, são suas mentes exercendo uma espécie de proteção
> contra o esforço cognitivo exacerbado, sussurrando aos seus ouvidos: 'eu quero evitar a fadiga'."

**Satisficing** (Steve Krug, *Don't Make Me Think!*): we don't pick the best option, we pick the
**first reasonable one**. Not optimal — adequate. Or what merely *appears* to meet the need.

**Paradox of Choice** (Barry Schwartz): "quanto mais opções você tiver, mais difícil será decidir."

### Evidence

| Study | Result |
|---|---|
| Parole hearings (1,000+ decisions analyzed) | **Time of day was the main predictor of approval.** Early hearings: **70%** granted parole. Late hearings: **10%**. Fatigue pushed judges to the default — denial. The behavior was not malicious; decision-making simply became erratic under mental load. |
| Ballot decision fatigue | With many options on the ballot, participants were more likely to abstain, vote the pre-selected option, or the first candidate. Without decision fatigue, **abstentions would drop 8%**. |
| Netflix choosing time (2,000 people) | **17.8 minutes** average to pick something, vs **9.1 minutes** for cable TV (whose catalogue is reduced to what's airing live). |
| Netflix recommendations | **80%+** of watched items come from Netflix's own recommendations. "Muitas opções, mas nem sempre quem escolhe somos nós." |
| Barry Schwartz | With many options, consumers are **less likely to buy anything**, and if they buy, they're **less satisfied** with their choice. Facing large sets of alternatives can produce anxiety, regret, excessively high expectations, and self-blame if the choice doesn't work out. |
| Long-list study | The further you scroll down a list, the more susceptible you become to picking the easiest answer — long menus, endless questionnaires. |

Also: technology multiplied not just the number of choices but the number of **external influences**
on our decisions (Greg McKeown, *Essencialismo*) — hyperconnection, distraction from information
overload, and other people's opinions about what we should focus on.

### The kiosk case — read the whole environment, not the screen

McDonald's replaced attendants with self-service kiosks. The author's complaint is precise: for a
simple Big Mac combo he must go to the offers/combos section, then define each item — which soda?
which size? regular or zero? small, medium or large fries? sauce? onion? lettuce? tomato? cheese?
**"A fome até passou."**

The kiosk isn't wrong to offer the options — a human attendant allowed the same ones. The
differences:

1. With a human, **you didn't see all the options at once** — many were *suggested* by the attendant.
2. **Speaking is faster and easier than scanning options on screen and tapping them.**

Same catalog, different cognitive load. And the book documents the outcome with a photo: a queue
generated by the automated ordering (Brazil, October 2022).

Adjacent research by the author: bank customers resisting ATMs weren't only elderly or unskilled —
young interviewees also avoided them, fearing a wrong transaction, **especially with people queuing
behind them or when amounts were higher**. The environment around the screen changed the decision.

This is the book's central design claim (Human Experience Design):

> "é preciso projetar pensando não somente na tela (a interface; o ponto de contato com o usuário),
> mas no todo, no ambiente do usuário, como o produto afetará sua vida ao redor, e como o redor
> impactará sua experiência ao utilizar o produto."

### Design moves

| Move | Case in the book |
|---|---|
| Cut the menu depth | **Zara** — single-level, lean menu; clean, scannable category and product pages, even at a larger product grid |
| Make items **highly distinguishable** | Zara again — a dozen sweatshirts, but each is distinctly different. "São únicos. E esse é um ponto que pode aliviar em nossas tomadas de decisão." |
| Anticipatory design | **Spotify** "Descubra algo novo pra você" / "Descobertas da Semana" — data science + ML + user research + interface design, integrated to deliver content based on the user's desires and needs |
| Recommendation algorithms | **Netflix** — because choice overload is so damaging to their UX |
| Reduce your own decisions | Zuckerberg's gray shirts — not fandom, just minimizing decisions |
| Filters, search, and information hierarchy | Where you can't remove options outright, structure them |
| Follow a decision process | *Solvable*: **Frame** (understand the problem) → **Explore** (identify alternatives and criteria) → **Decide** (select the best choice). Good design can follow these steps based on user needs. |

### The counter-argument the author addresses head-on

He publicly analyzed a sales site and recommended **removing** options from the product detail page
to maximize sales and retention. Viewers pushed back: *remove options from a sales site? That would
hurt revenue!*

His answer: it **may** be true in specific cases — AliExpress works like a Chinese flea market where
hunting products you'll never use is part of the fun. But Schwartz's research says the general case
runs the other way. And when you can't remove options, use smart screens that assist the choice.

**Banana Republic** is his negative case: elegant, huge professional images — and a category page
where the shown fragment is only ~20% of the page, plus an extensive, deep menu. "Parece ter sido
projetada para desgastar o scroll do mouse." Fine for browsing; punishing if you actually want to buy
one item.

### Failure mode

Recommendation algorithms solve overload by **deciding for the user** — "corremos o risco de viver
em uma bolha". The author notes Spotify still thinks he likes Shania Twain after half a decade. The
fix for overload can quietly become a different problem.

## 2. Affect Heuristic — "our decisions aren't purely rational, they're influenced by emotions"

### Mechanism

A mental shortcut where we rely on **emotions instead of concrete information**, especially under
time pressure. In Kahneman's terms (*Noise*), heuristics come from fast, intuitive System 1 thinking
— often useful, sometimes producing biases (systematic, predictable judgment errors).

Plainly: **how you feel about a stimulus influences the decision you make about it.** Faster and
easier — and potentially distorted into non-ideal choices.

**Schwarz's four conditions** that make someone use emotion as a decision shortcut:

1. When the judgment is **affective** — e.g. liking or trusting someone
2. When **little other information** is available
3. When the judgment is **complicated and complex** to make from ordinary information
4. When **time is limited**

So: the more complex the decision, the higher the chance of acting on feeling. This connects
directly to Decision Fatigue above.

Mood matters too: in a **positive emotional state**, you're more likely to perceive something as
having **more benefits and lower risks**.

Related heuristics named: Availability, Representativeness, and the **Halo Effect** (the book's
example: a politician convicted of corruption returns in navy blue with simple glasses and a gentler
tone — and you re-rate his character, ignoring his history).

### Evidence

| Study | Result |
|---|---|
| Netflix thumbnails (2014 research) | Thumbnails were the **biggest influencer** of the decision to watch, and **82% of user focus** while browsing. Subscribers spent an average **1.8 seconds** per title. The thumbnail — not the trailer, not the synopsis — dictated the choice. |
| Netflix, faces | Humans naturally respond to faces. Faces with **complex emotions outperform** smiling or expressionless ones — likely because complex emotions convey richer detail about the content's mood or style. ("Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt" winning image.) |
| Proportion Dominance Effect (ice cream) | Two pots, both with 7 scoops: a large one not full, a small one overflowing. People were **much more willing to pay for the smaller pot** — it looked like more ice cream. |
| Risk framing to clinicians | Recidivism as probabilities ("20% chance") vs frequencies ("20 in 100 patients") → clinicians rated patients as **higher risk** with frequencies. Frequencies create a **mental image** of the individual reoffending. |
| Canada — cigarette warning labels | **20%** said they smoked less after seeing packs with striking disease imagery (e.g. lung cancer). |
| Book covers | Women 18–35 preferred **cool-colored** covers; men 56+ preferred **warm** colors. Not observed in younger age brackets. |
| Book covers, pupils | Same book, same cover photo of a woman, pupils digitally enlarged on one. A significantly higher percentage of **men** chose the enlarged-pupil cover — inconsistently. (The author flags this one as "controverso e bizarro".) |

### Design moves

| Move | Case in the book |
|---|---|
| Lead with imagery that hits the audience's actual emotion | **Airbnb** — 2021 homepage was a search form with filters and one big background illustration; by 2022 it opened straight into containers, motorhomes and treehouses. Their audience averages ~30 years old and looks for unusual stays. In December 2019, nearly 3,000 people stayed in **castles**. |
| Invest in the thumbnail as the actual decision surface | Netflix — 82% of focus, 1.8s per title |
| Present quantities so the perception matches the value | The ice-cream pots: shape and fill change willingness to pay at identical quantity |
| Affective memory as product strategy | Disney: **US$56.2bn** in licensed retail worldwide in 2021 (Pixar, Marvel, Star Wars) |

The author's own worked example is his car history: Fiat Uno Vivace → Jeep Renegade → Willys CJ5 →
Wrangler. All share straight lines, sharp corners, imposing aesthetics, unique design. **"Nunca me
importei com o motor, com a autonomia de combustível, ou com outras qualidades de cunho técnico."**
Two forces drove every purchase: money and emotion — the emotion traced to 1980s/90s childhood
(G.I. Joe, Rambo, Robocop, Terminator). Whereas the Palio and the Prisma were objectively fine cars:
*"Sem sal. Nunca gostei tanto."*

His counter-example is honest about the downside: he and his fiancée bought Big Brother
Brasil–branded deodorant purely because they liked the show, without testing it. It was terrible.

The takeaway the chapter closes on: **"Conhecer o usuário e saber o que mexe com suas emoções pode
ser um excelente caminho para um produto ou serviço de sucesso."**
