## Management Fundamentals

**Golden rule**: Do what it takes to get the best out of your team. Your value is no longer your personal output but the output of your team.

**Kaizen (Continuous Improvement)**: Borrowed from Toyota Production System. Provide your team with a "red handle" — empower everyone to stop and fix process issues. Treat mistakes as improvement opportunities, not failures.

**Key mindset shifts for new managers**:
- Find a management mentor or coach
- Learn to delegate (your value = team output, not personal output)
- Take care of yourself — managing people is emotionally draining
- Expect a lifelong learning journey in management

## Coaching

Your principal role is coach, not manager. A coach is on your team's side, provides wisdom and guidance, gives critical feedback quickly, and celebrates success first.

**Coaching managers**: As the org grows, effective middle managers become critical. Build:
- A culture of continuous learning (management book clubs, #management-insights channels)
- A high bar for coaching expectations (codified in internal docs)
- Thorough management training materials (subscriptions, conferences, coaches)
- An external-facing culture of thought leadership

**Coaching engineers**: Engineers should vent to you regularly — that's healthy. Have 1:1s at least biweekly, ideally weekly. Your job: listen, ask clarifying questions, demonstrate understanding, steer toward solutions. Rarely solve their problems directly.

## 1:1 Meetings

**Purpose**: Build relationships, have vulnerable/critical conversations that enable best work. NOT just status check-ins.

**Content framework**: Use a shared document with structure/templates. Include prompts for:
- Liked/wished-for items at personal, departmental, and company levels
- Bidirectional feedback between manager and employee
- Conflict (within team, across teams, cross-functional)
- Performance and development
- Clarity (seeking perspectives or information)
- Context (broader company goals, how work connects)

**Skip-level meetings**: Monthly or quarterly meetings with direct reports of your managers. Purpose: build relationships, hear insights on leadership/culture/strategy. Put the employee at ease — you're not there to undermine their manager.

**Radical Candor** (Kim Scott): Communication that "cares personally while challenging directly." Contrast with:
- **Obnoxious Aggression**: Direct challenge without caring
- **Ruinous Empathy**: Caring without challenging
- **Manipulative Insincerity**: Neither caring nor challenging

## Communication

### Overcommunication
Default to public channels. In the absence of information, people assume worst-case scenarios. Overcommunication's worst consequence (redundancy) is easily fixed; undercommunication's consequences (anxiety, confusion) are not.

### Email Best Practices
- Don't let email become your job — check at fixed times, disable phone notifications
- Reach inbox zero daily (triage, don't necessarily act on everything)
- Don't problem-solve in email — use meetings or memos for nuanced discussions
- Keep emails short, bulleted, with bold/highlighting for action items
- Be mindful of audience — engineers prefer code over email

### Synchronous Chat (Slack/Teams)
- Include all necessary context in each message (minimize back-and-forth)
- Use formatting (bullets, headings) for longer messages
- Centralize conversations in channels/threads
- Use DND/notification schedules
- Default to public channels
- Be judicious with @here/@channel

### Asynchronous Communication
- Initial message must be a complete thought with full context
- Replace meetings where possible — leaves written documentation
- Example: "Feature is broken" (bad) vs. full reproduction steps + video (good)

### Documentation
- Live the value yourself — write wiki articles and share links
- Build documentation into process (onboarding, specs, RFCs, PRs)
- Maintain documentation freshness — "last updated" dates
- Boy Scout Rule: leave docs better than you found them
- Every repo needs README.md with: Installation, Directory Structure, Development loop, Deployment

### Acronyms Policy
Discourage made-up acronyms. They create barriers for new employees and obscure communication. Industry-standard acronyms (GUI, API) are fine. Get executive buy-in, gentle nudge in onboarding docs.

## Meetings and Time Management

**Three types**: Informational (consider if meeting is best medium), conflict resolution (identify discussion points in advance), ad-hoc (clear purpose).

**Time management**: Invest in systems — recommend "7 Habits" (Covey) and "Getting Things Done" (Allen).

**Meeting timing**: Create "meeting hours" windows (2-3 hours daily). Keep engineers out of meetings outside this window. If engineers are in >3 hours of meetings/day, consolidate.

**Engineer's typical week** (hypothetical):
- 1h 1:1 + 2.5h standups + 2h agile ceremonies + 4-8h code review + 4h email/chat = 13-17h
- Add context switching → only ~20h for actual engineering
- Providing large blocks of focus time does NOT happen by accident.

**HIPPO effect**: Highest-Paid Person's Opinion. Your title suppresses others' ideas. Regularly open the door for challenges, champion others' ideas, attend meetings only when you truly add value.

**To-do lists**: Use calendar-based tasks instead of generic lists. Blocks time, enables prioritization, provides reminders.

**Calendar retrospectives**: Monthly, review how time was spent. Google Calendar has built-in analytics.

## Joining a Team

### Promoted into Management
- Technical skills = prerequisite, not sufficient
- Moving from C to managing people is a bigger shift than C to TypeScript
- Invest in management learning: books, mentors, coaches, user groups

### Hired to Lead
- Focus on people over technology initially
- Short-term goals:
  - Build trust with tech team (listen before changing)
  - Build trust with other leaders (make and meet commitments)
  - Learn people's history with company/product
  - Diagnose highest-impact people challenges (leveling, culture)
  - Diagnose highest-impact technical problems
- Key questions:
  - Who ran tech before? Still on the team?
  - What does the CEO say you were hired to solve?
  - What do YOU think you were hired to solve?
  - Pain points between tech team and rest of company?
  - Highest-priority internal pain points?

## Working with the CEO

### Alignment Areas
1. **Specific objectives**: CEO must trust your ability to lead. Speak business language, avoid tech jargon.
2. **Business direction**: Shared understanding. Build trust via non-work activities, shared values, tools like BRAVING Inventory.
3. **Culture and values**: Strong alignment reduces turnover. Tech is often the largest budget line item.

### Your "First Team"
Your first team is the leadership/C-suite, not the engineering team. Don't let technical shiny objects distract from investing in non-technical peer relationships. A CTO who only codes with engineers will lack credibility when advocating for engineering investment.

### Delivering Bad News
Don't sugarcoat. If deadlines slip or quality drops, peers will notice the gap between your words and reality. Honesty builds trust needed for future asks (tech debt investment, architecture rewrites).

### Speaking to Non-Technical Audiences
- Establish shared vocabulary upfront
- Use relatable concepts (traffic on a highway, not bytes per second)
- Confirm understanding along the way
- Don't make audience feel inferior
- Keep explanations simple and concise

## Hiring and Interviewing

### Startup Advantages
- Speed (week-long processes vs. months at big companies)
- Compelling growth trajectory
- Creative workplace culture
- Impact and equity ownership

### Practical Tips
- Train interviewers BEFORE posting the job
- Schedule ALL interviews upfront (within 5 business days)
- Nobody is too important to meet strong candidates
- Each interviewer gets unique script covering different material
- Be candid about runway, product-market fit, culture

### Headcount Planning
Consider: need/team gap, prioritization/timing, budget. Types of gaps: skill, seniority, management, subject matter expertise, bandwidth.

### Hiring Goals
1. **Efficiency**: Time and cost to hire
2. **Success rate**: Track retention, new hire attrition, promotions in first 2 years
3. **Candidate experience**: Even rejected candidates should leave positive reviews
4. **Equitable opportunities**: Rubric-based evaluation, avoid unconscious bias
5. **Scalability**: Others can run the process with same efficacy

### Job Description
- Create clarity on purpose, outcomes, and impact (3/6/12 months)
- Socialize with other leaders
- Include core values, team structure, compensation, leveling, location requirements
- It's an advertisement for your culture and brand

### Sourcing
- **Inbound**: Job boards, community promotion, brand marketing
- **Outbound**: Start in-house first to learn the market, then use external recruiters
- **Referrals**: Highest ROI. Multi-thousand-dollar bonuses justified by cost savings. Consider abbreviated process.

### Interview Process
- **Intake forms**: Company info + qualifying questions. Include culture sample.
- **Phone screen**: Company intro, structural fit check, compensation alignment. Doesn't need to be technical.
- **Culture interview (Topgrading)**: Walk through employment history. For each role ask: successes, mistakes, supervisor's name, their assessment of you, your assessment of them.
- **Coding challenge**: Must exist. Time-limit it, relate to your business domain, provide starter repos, be explicit about evaluation criteria.
- **Technical interview**: Use a "technical focus interview guide" with 4-8 technical areas, sample questions, best practice answers, and scoring guide.
- **Reference checks**: Ask for specific examples, not just endorsements.

### Offer and Compensation
- Move fast — speed is your friend
- Include base salary, equity, benefits, start date, title, reporting structure
- Sell the opportunity, not just the compensation

## Onboarding

**Goals**: New hires should feel welcomed, understand the company, and be productive quickly.

**Key elements**:
- Assign a buddy/mentor for first 30 days
- Provide clear documentation (README, wiki, onboarding checklist)
- Formal check-ins at 15, 30, 60, and 90 days
- Consider a 90-day "probationary" period with structured evaluations
- Onboarding is everybody's responsibility — document tribal knowledge continuously

## Performance Management

### Performance Reviews
- Use a formal, documented system
- Reviews should be bidirectional (manager ↔ employee)
- Include self-assessment, peer feedback, manager assessment
- Tie to clear expectations set in advance
- Frequency: formal reviews semi-annually or annually, with continuous informal feedback

### Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs)
- Document clearly what needs to improve, by when, with what support
- PIPs should not be surprises — issues should be raised in 1:1s first
- Set measurable, time-bound goals
- Genuinely try to help the person succeed

### Dealing with Toxic Behavior
Every day toxic behavior persists increases team resentment toward management. No amount of individual productivity compensates for culture damage and management credibility loss.

### Firing
- Follow company procedure meticulously
- Documentation: Sufficient justification, for-cause records
- Timing: Do it soon after deciding. First of month = extra healthcare month.
- Witnesses: Manager + HR present. Keep meeting short.
- Offboarding: Plan access revocation and hardware recovery in advance.
- Severance: Standard packages (weeks for ICs, months for executives), factoring tenure. Letting someone go is often management's failure too.

## Team Makeup

### Seniority Mix
Weight toward senior talent when codebase is: very new (greenfield), very old (brownfield), changing requirements significantly, using new tools/patterns, or establishing new patterns.

### Specialization by Team Size
- **1-5**: All generalists (frontend/backend/mobile at most)
- **5-15**: Specialized by product or skill area. Start thinking about dedicated testing/DevOps.
- **15-30**: Real specialization. Invest in developer experience and operational efficiency.
- **30+**: Break into pods (cross-functional teams aligned to product areas). Many methodologies exist.

### Two Crews Model (Microsoft)
- **Feature Crew**: Builds new features (future-focused)
- **Customer Crew**: Handles support, bugs, site health (present-focused)
- Benefits: dedicated monitoring, undistracted feature team, specialization, career path for junior engineers
- Rotation options: permanent team, career trajectory (12+ months then transfer), weekly rotation, or temporary team

### Team Organization
- **Functional**: Organized by type of work (frontend, testing). Creates silos.
- **Product (Pods)**: Organized by business area. Cross-functional, autonomous, accountable. Right answer most of the time.
- Pods bring: cohesion, autonomy, ownership, velocity. Tradeoff: knowledge silos, potential for local optimization.

### Managing Remote Teams
- **Documentation**: Robust internal docs with search — substitute for asking the person next to you
- **Async-first**: Lean into async practices. Reduces timezone burden, fewer meetings.
- **In-person meetups**: Quarterly minimum. Bonds formed in-person endure for months.
- **Time zone overlap**: Minimum 4 working hours overlap for teams on same project.
- **Social opportunities**: Icebreaker questions, virtual happy hours, remote social events.
- **Camera on**: 70-90% of communication is nonverbal. Zero tolerance for appearance remarks.
- **Recording meetings**: Allows async consumption, reduces meeting headcount pressure.

## Leadership Responsibilities

### Product and Design Teams
- Don't throw things over walls. Empathy and understanding between EPD (Engineering, Product, Design).
- **Design systems**: Use off-the-shelf (Material, AntD, Chakra, Bootstrap). Integrates designer tools (Figma) with engineering components (React, Angular).
- **PRDs**: Describe problem background and "why". Living documents. Leave "how" to technical specs.
- Formalize project management when EPD reaches ~20 people.

### Finances and Budgeting
- Know your department's contribution to the financial model
- Maintain a spreadsheet: Payroll, SaaS/COGS, Infrastructure, Other, Summary
- Burden rate: actual employee cost ≈ salary × 1.2
- Help CFO avoid surprises: forecast hiring, SaaS growth, cloud costs
- Track SaaS with credit card analysis tools or SaaS Management Platforms (SMPs)

### Measuring Engineering Velocity
- Directly quantifying output is extremely difficult
- Measure **cycle time**: coding → code review → approval → deployment
- Measure **work time allocation**: what types of work consume time (features vs bugs vs debt)
- Tools: LinearB, Code Climate

### Fundraising & Due Diligence
Be ready to produce: org chart, department budget, product descriptions, roadmaps, tech debt inventory, architecture diagrams, deployment description, security practices, software licensing info. Be candid with auditors.

### Vendor Management
- Self-service tools below cost threshold: let managers sign up independently
- Enterprise: research space first, build BATNA, delegate sales qualification calls
- Negotiation tips: be transparent about needs, value contract length/payment terms, "grow together" pricing, leverage end-of-quarter quotas
- Post-sales: build relationship with CSM for advocacy on features/defects
